Section 02 · Global Issue

Heritage vs. Modernity

Bodrum is a city pulling in two directions at once. One side is ancient — ruins, old fishing docks, stone walls that have been standing for thousands of years. The other side is brand-new luxury. Our research question was simple: what gets lost when one takes over the other?

Heritage Preservation Maritime Culture Sustainable Tourism
Ancient ruins a short walk from a superyacht marina
2,500+
years of recorded history in Bodrum — most people just go there for the beach
  • 🏛️
    Ancient Sites That Are Actually at Risk
    The Mausoleum at Halicarnassus was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World — and today most of it is gone. Myndos Gate, where Alexander the Great laid siege in 334 BC, sits at the edge of a modern neighborhood. We wanted to understand what it takes to protect places like these when the land around them keeps getting developed.
  • ⚓
    What Happens to the Fishing Villages
    Yalıkavak used to be a quiet fishing town. Now it has one of the most expensive marinas in Turkey. The boats are still there, but the fishermen who used to sell their catch on the dock have mostly moved on. Walking through both parts of that town in the same afternoon tells you a lot about who development actually benefits.
  • 🌊
    A Museum Built Inside a Castle
    The Bodrum Museum of Underwater Archaeology is housed inside a Crusader castle from the 1400s. Inside it, they've preserved Bronze Age shipwrecks, ancient glassware, and artifacts pulled from the seabed nearby. It's one of those places that makes you realize how much history is sitting right beneath the surface — literally.
  • 🧭
    Going as Observers, Not Just Tourists
    The goal wasn't to show up, take pictures, and leave. We went with actual questions we wanted to think through — and we tried to pay attention in a way that most trips don't push you to. That meant talking to people, slowing down at sites that weren't on the main tourist circuit, and writing things down while they were still fresh.

Section 04 · Dining Profiles

Where We Ate

We didn't want to just eat at random places. Every spot was picked because it said something about the area — whether that was a restaurant that's been on the harbor since the 90s or a terrace so high up you can see the whole peninsula.

Breakfasts

Limon Gümüşlük
Sits up on a hillside above the bay with a garden that catches the morning sun. We had breakfast here looking out at the water through olive trees — probably the most relaxed start to any day on the trip.
Breakfast
Teldolap Café & Restaurant
A cliffside terrace with a serious view. The kind of place where you order something simple and then just sit there for way longer than you planned because no one wants to leave.
Breakfast

Lunches

Avlu Bodrum
Hidden inside an old stone building right in the centre of town. The courtyard is shaded and cool even in the afternoon heat — you'd walk past the entrance ten times without knowing it was there.
Lunch
Antik Akdeniz
Wood-fired pides, proper ones. This is the kind of place locals actually eat at — nothing fancy, just good food at honest prices in the middle of the old town.
Lunch
Kocadon Restaurant
A 19th-century villa that was turned into a restaurant at some point, and it shows — stone archways, vines growing overhead, the whole thing. The mezze spread here was genuinely one of the best meals of the week.
Lunch
Sünger Pizza
Been open since 1992, right on the harbour. Half the town seems to eat here. The pizza is thin, crispy, and nothing like what you'd expect — in the best way.
Lunch
Kitchenette Yalıkavak
A café-style stop at the marina. Good for a midday break when the group needed to sit down, eat something decent, and figure out what we were doing next.
Lunch
La Pasion Cafe
A small courtyard tucked into one of Bodrum's quieter side streets. Colourful walls, mismatched furniture, really good food. It felt more like eating at someone's house than at a restaurant.
Lunch

Dinners

Frenk Bodrum
Right on the harbour, busy and loud in the best way. Good spot for the first night — it eased the group into Bodrum before we started getting into the heavier historical stuff the next morning.
Dinner
Aquarium Balık Restaurant
You eat fish here with sunken ancient ruins visible right next to your table in the water. That's not a metaphor — the ruins are genuinely just there, a few metres away. One of the stranger and more memorable meals of the trip.
Dinner
Yarbasan Restaurant
Built to look like a traditional Turkish stone village — individual buildings, winding paths between them, lanterns everywhere. It sounds like a theme park but it actually works. The food is serious and the atmosphere is unlike anywhere else.
Dinner
Windmills Dağbelen
Up on a mountain ridge above the city. You can see the whole peninsula from up there — the bays, the islands, the marina lights in the distance. We went for sunset and nobody wanted to come back down.
Dinner
Sait Restaurant
Waterfront at Yalıkavak Marina. Upscale, calm, unhurried. A good night to sit down as a group after a full day and actually process what we'd been seeing all week.
Dinner
Berk Balık Restaurant
Right on the beach, facing back towards the castle. At night the whole fortress is lit up and it reflects across the harbour. We saved this one for the last evening — it felt like the right way to end the trip.
Dinner
